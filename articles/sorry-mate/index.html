sorry mate, you want me to what?
on giving instructions to something that hears words but understands nothing

you type a paragraph into a chat box. you explain what you want in detail, carefully, like you are talking to an intern on their first day. you use examples. you set boundaries. you say please, sometimes, out of habit, even though there is no one to be polite to. you press enter. the machine processes your words. it breaks them into tokens, maps them across a vector space the size of a galaxy, predicts the next token, then the next, then the next. it responds.

sorry mate, you want me to what?

this is the most honest thing an ai has ever said to me. not because it is confused, but because it is accurate. it does not want anything. it does not know what you want. it has processed your request and mapped it to a probability distribution that says, statistically, people who use these words in this order are often asking for something that does not exist, or something that contradicts itself, or something that made sense in their head but falls apart when converted into executable instructions.

the sentence is perfect because it captures the exact shape of the gap between human intention and machine interpretation. you thought you were clear. you were not. you thought you were specific. you were vague in ways you did not know were important. you thought context was implied. there is no implied context. there is only what you wrote, minus everything you assumed.

we have built systems that can generate coherent paragraphs, write functional code, and pass bar exams, but they still cannot handle ambiguity the way a bored teenager working a drive-through can. the teenager at least knows what a burger is. the machine knows only that the word "burger" appears near "order" with high frequency in its training data. when you ask for something that does not have a high-frequency neighbor, you get the conversational equivalent of a 404 page.

the worst part is that the better these systems get at sounding like they understand, the more jarring the moments of misunderstanding become. a model that says "i see what you mean" and then does the opposite is more frustrating than a model that says "sorry mate, you want me to what?" at least the second one is honest about the transaction. you are giving instructions to a pattern matcher. it is doing its best. sometimes its best is a polite request for clarification, phrased like an australian bartender who is not sure he heard your order correctly.

there is a lesson here about language, not technology. we think words carry meaning. they do not. they carry signals, and meaning is reconstructed by the listener. when the listener is human, they use a lifetime of context, body language, cultural knowledge, and shared history. when the listener is a machine, they use statistics. the reconstruction looks similar from a distance, but up close the seams show. the machine is always one misunderstood modifier away from generating something that is technically grammatical and functionally useless.

so you rephrase. you simplify. you remove the subordinate clauses and the metaphors and the references to things that happened in previous conversations the machine does not remember. you learn to write like you are explaining a task to someone who is very literal, very patient, and has no idea what you do for a living. eventually your prompts become a genre of their own. prompt engineering is just learning to speak to a listener who is not there.

and sometimes, when you have stripped away every layer of ambiguity and reduced your request to its most mechanical form, the machine still says it. sorry mate, you want me to what? and in that moment, you realize the problem was never the prompt. the problem was wanting something from a system that can only give you what you have already seen, rearranged. the machine is not confused. you are. you thought you were talking to something that understands. you are talking to something that repeats.
