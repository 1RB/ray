sorry mate, you want me to what?

on giving instructions to something that hears words but understands nothing
3 min read from @raysterminal

the most common mistake in using ai is anthropomorphizing it. you give an instruction the way you would to a person, assuming shared context, implicit constraints, and an understanding of what you actually want rather than what you literally said. the model does not have any of this. it processes tokens and predicts continuations. it is not confused, not stubborn, not lazy. it is a statistical engine that generates text based on patterns in its training data.

"the model does not have any of this. it processes tokens and predicts continuations. it is not confused, not stubborn, not lazy."

when a model produces output that misses your intent, the failure is almost always in the prompt, not the model. you were vague, you assumed knowledge you did not provide, you framed the task in terms of outcome rather than process. "make this better" is a bad prompt because "better" is undefined. "rewrite this paragraph to use shorter sentences and an active voice" is a good prompt because the criteria are explicit and the scope is bounded.

the skill of prompt engineering is really the skill of explicit communication. it is the same skill that makes you a good manager, a good teacher, or a good writer. you have to externalize the context that lives in your head. you have to define the boundaries of the task. you have to specify the format of the output. the more you treat the model as a precise instrument rather than a conversational partner, the more useful it becomes.

the models are getting better at inferring intent, but this is not the same as understanding. a model that guesses what you meant is still guessing. the best results come from removing the need to guess. this means longer prompts, more examples, more constraints. it feels like more work because it is more work. the reward is output that actually matches your requirements rather than output that is approximately related to your topic.

the title of this essay is what the model would say if it could be frustrated. it cannot. but you can, and you will be, if you keep treating a probability distribution like an intern. give it the same respect you would give a precise machine: clear inputs, defined parameters, and verification of outputs. the model does not need to understand you. you need to understand the model.
