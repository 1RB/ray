vibe coding straight to your eyeballs

realities terminal. the vr headset that runs ai in your field of view.
4 min read from @raysterminal
standalone vr on-device ai vibe coding passthrough

realities terminal is a standalone vr headset with on-device generative ai. it is not a concept. it is hardware you can wear, and when you do, the ai is not on a screen in front of you. it is in your field of view. you look at something, describe what you want, and the model generates it in the space where you are standing. this is not a phone app with a camera overlay. this is a compute unit strapped to your face, running inference locally, projecting results into stereoscopic 3d.

the headset runs a fork of android with a custom rendering layer. the ai model is quantized to run on the headset's own gpu, not in the cloud. the latency is low enough that the generation feels responsive, not like sending a prompt to a server and waiting. you speak, you see. the passthrough cameras let you anchor generated objects to real surfaces, so the ai output composites with your actual environment.

the use case the team pushes hardest is "vibe coding": describing a scene, a character, an environment, or an interface, and having it appear in front of you. you iterate by talking. "make the building taller," "change the sky to sunset," "add a second character." the model updates the scene. you are not typing code or adjusting sliders. you are describing, and the headset is interpreting.

the hardware is early. the field of view is narrower than high-end vr headsets. the resolution is sufficient for prototyping but not for final production. the battery lasts approximately ninety minutes under full ai load. the device gets warm. none of this is hidden. the product is aimed at developers and artists who want to iterate in spatial context, not at consumers who want a polished entertainment experience.

the implications are more interesting than the product. this is the first commercially available headset that runs generative ai fully on-device with passthrough compositing. it establishes a baseline for what local spatial ai looks like. future headsets will have better chips, better optics, and better models. but the interaction pattern, speaking to generate in your field of view, is likely to persist. it is the most natural interface for spatial computing because it requires no controllers, no keyboards, and no prior knowledge of 3d software.

the price has not been announced. the release date has not been announced. the company is taking developer applications for early units. based on the hardware cost and the market for similar devices, the final price will likely be between five hundred and one thousand dollars. expensive for a toy, cheap for a prototyping tool. the decision to buy will depend on whether you have a use case that requires seeing ai output in spatial context rather than on a screen.
