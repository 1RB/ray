YC summer 2026 startup wishlist
what the biggest accelerator wants to fund this year

every year y combinator publishes a list of ideas it wants founders to build. the summer 2026 edition is now live at ycombinator.com/rfs and it is worth reading closely. not because these are the only ideas they will fund, but because the document maps where the partners think the frontier has moved.

this year the preamble makes the shift explicit: ai has stopped being a feature and started being the foundation. the wishlist is less about bolting chatbots onto existing products and more about rebuilding entire industries with ai as the substrate. several entries come directly from yc founders describing what they are seeing on the frontier, which gives the document a different texture than prior years. less speculative. more operational.

ai in the physical world

low-pesticide agriculture
modern farming runs on chemicals that are losing efficacy. cheap sensors, computer vision, and precision robotics can identify individual weeds and pests. the ask is for startups that cut pesticide use by ninety percent while increasing yields.
counter-swarm defense
a thousand cheap drones coordinate an attack and current defenses are too expensive or too slow. the request is for high-capacity interceptors, sensor-fusing software, and non-kinetic defenses. "the winning companies will look more like cloudflare than raytheon."

silicon and supply chains

supply chain 2.0 for semiconductors
a single advanced ai chip crosses a dozen countries and fourteen hundred process steps, managed with spreadsheets and sap. the 2021 chip shortage cost the auto industry two hundred ten billion dollars. looking for real-time allocation tracking, multi-tier risk monitoring, and export compliance tools.
inference chips for agent workflows
current gpus achieve thirty to forty percent utilization on agentic workloads because the work is bursty. nvidia bought groq for twenty billion; google built tpu v7 specifically for inference. the request is purpose-built silicon for the agent loop itself.
electronics in space
inference chips optimized for mass, thermal, and radiation, intended for deployment in orbit once reusable rockets dramatically increase launch capacity.

software rebuilt

saas challengers
ai has collapsed the cost of producing software by ten to one hundred times, eroding the moat of legacy codebases. founders should attack products that "seem invulnerable": chip design software, erps, industrial control systems.
ai-native service companies
instead of selling software that helps people do their jobs, the startup should sell the service itself. target areas include insurance brokerage, accounting, tax, audit, compliance, and healthcare administration.
software for agents
the next trillion users on the internet will be ai agents, not people. agents need machine-readable interfaces, apis, and documentation so they can discover and use tools without human intervention. "make something agents want."

the company stack

company brain
the blocker to company-wide ai automation is no longer the models but the domain knowledge scattered across slack threads, support tickets, email archives, and databases. the request is for a living map of how a company works.
the ai operating system for companies
the connective layer that makes a company legible to ai by default, with every meeting recorded, every ticket tracked, and a single intelligence layer that flags when engineering is building the wrong thing.
dynamic software interfaces
if coding agents are now capable enough, users can become their own forward deployed engineers, customizing interfaces radically while sharing underlying primitives.

medicine and science

ai personalized medicine
agents that analyze personalized health data, the plummeting cost of personalized diagnostics, and the falling cost of n-of-one genetic therapies delivered via mrna. the fda has also expressed more openness to letting patients try these procedures.
ai-native discovery engines
systems that run the full design-make-test-analyze loop in drug discovery, materials science, and protein engineering. the frontier is shifting from copilot assistants to closed-loop discovery systems that propose and validate hypotheses autonomously.

compared to prior lists, the summer 2026 edition is heavier on hardware, defense, and semiconductors. entries on space manufacturing, drone swarms, and chip supply chains would have been out of place two years ago. the shift reflects a broader move at yc toward funding hard-tech companies and the recognition that building hardware in the united states is still far slower than in shenzhen.

yc says these requests are "just a fraction of what we fund," and that you do not need to work on these ideas to apply. that is technically true, but the list functions as a signal amplification device. if an idea appears here, multiple partners have thought about it, know the market size, and can evaluate a pitch without needing a primer. for founders, that reduces friction. for everyone else, it is a barometer of where the frontier has moved.
