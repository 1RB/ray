YC summer 2026 startup wishlist

what the biggest accelerator wants to fund this year

every year y combinator publishes a list of ideas it wants founders to build. the summer 2026 edition is now live at ycombinator.com/rfs and it is worth reading closely. not because these are the only ideas they will fund, but because the document maps where the partners think the frontier has moved.

this year the preamble makes the shift explicit: ai has stopped being a feature and started being the foundation. the wishlist is less about bolting chatbots onto existing products and more about rebuilding entire industries with ai as the substrate. several entries come directly from yc founders describing what they are seeing on the frontier, which gives the document a different texture than prior years. less speculative. more operational.

"ai has stopped being a feature and started being the foundation."

the sixteen requests fall into rough categories. here is what they are asking for, in plain terms.

ai in the physical world. garry tan's entry on low-pesticide agriculture opens with a blunt premise: modern farming runs on chemicals that are losing efficacy, and farmers are trapped in a cost spiral. cheap sensors, computer vision, and precision robotics can now identify individual weeds and pests. the ask is for startups that cut pesticide use by ninety percent while increasing yields. tyler bosmeny's counter-swarm defense describes a future in which a thousand cheap drones coordinate an attack and current defenses are too expensive or too slow to matter. the comparison point is deliberate: "the winning companies will look more like cloudflare than raytheon."

silicon and supply chains. diana hu has three entries this year, two of them about chips. supply chain 2.0 for semiconductors notes that a single advanced ai chip crosses a dozen countries and fourteen hundred process steps, managed with spreadsheets and sap. the 2021 chip shortage cost the auto industry two hundred ten billion dollars. hu is looking for real-time allocation tracking, multi-tier risk monitoring, and export compliance tools. the second entry, inference chips for agent workflows, points out that current gpus achieve thirty to forty percent utilization on agentic workloads because the work is bursty. nvidia bought groq for twenty billion; google built tpu v7 specifically for inference. hu wants purpose-built silicon for the agent loop itself.

software rebuilt. jared friedman's saas challengers frames incumbents as vulnerable. ai has collapsed the cost of producing software by ten to one hundred times, eroding the moat of legacy codebases built over decades. friedman encourages founders to attack products that "seem invulnerable": chip design software, erps, industrial control systems. gustaf alstromer's ai-native service companies pushes the logic further. instead of selling software that helps people do their jobs, the startup should sell the service itself. the total spend on services dwarfs software spend. aaron epstein's software for agents flips the user: the next trillion users on the internet will be ai agents, not people.

the company stack. tom blomfield's company brain argues that the blocker to company-wide ai automation is no longer the models but the domain knowledge scattered across slack threads, support tickets, email archives, and databases. the request is for a living map of how a company works. diana hu's third entry, the ai operating system for companies, is adjacent: the connective layer that makes a company legible to ai by default.

medicine and science. ankit gupta's ai personalized medicine ties together agents that analyze personalized health data, the plummeting cost of personalized diagnostics, and the falling cost of n-of-one genetic therapies delivered via mrna. jon xu's ai-native discovery engines is broader: systems that run the full design-make-test-analyze loop in drug discovery, materials science, and protein engineering. the frontier, xu argues, is shifting from copilot assistants to closed-loop discovery systems that propose and validate hypotheses autonomously.

compared to prior lists, the summer 2026 edition is heavier on hardware, defense, and semiconductors. entries on space manufacturing, drone swarms, and chip supply chains would have been out of place two years ago. the shift reflects a broader move at yc toward funding hard-tech companies and the recognition that building hardware in the united states is still far slower than in shenzhen.

another change is the tone toward enterprise sales. harshita arora and brad flora's entry, startups that want to sell to huge companies, marks a departure from paul graham's classic advice to sell to other startups. the partners now argue that fortune one hundred buyers are actively looking for ai teams to solve key problems, and in the last three years yc companies have landed multimillion-dollar deals with fortune ten customers within their first year. the entry is essentially a permission slip to go after giant customers early.

yc says these requests are "just a fraction of what we fund," and that you do not need to work on these ideas to apply. that is technically true, but the list functions as a signal amplification device. if an idea appears here, multiple partners have thought about it, know the market size, and can evaluate a pitch without needing a primer. for founders, that reduces friction. for everyone else, it is a barometer of where the frontier has moved. from ai as a feature, to ai as the foundation, to ai as the entire stack.
