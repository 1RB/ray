the bottleneck was never the learner

joe liemandt on the knowledge project
4 min read from @raysterminal

joe liemandt, on the knowledge project, made a claim that sounds like hyperbole until you think about it. a kid can master seventh grade science in twenty-two hours. the same kid, in the same school system, takes over two hundred hours to cover the same material. same child, same brain, same subject. completely different outcomes. the bottleneck, liemandt says, was never the learner. it was the system.

the twenty-two hour figure comes from competency-based experiments where students learn at their own pace, test when ready, and move on only when they have demonstrated mastery. the two hundred plus hours is the standard american school year: fifty-minute periods, five days a week, thirty-six weeks, minus assemblies, holidays, standardized test prep, substitute teacher days, and the twenty minutes at the start of every class spent taking attendance and waiting for students to settle. the ratio is not ten to one because students in the traditional system learn ten times slower. it is ten to one because the traditional system is doing ten different things, only one of which is teaching science.

same material, same kid, completely different outcomes. the bottleneck was never the learner.

the school system was designed for an era when information was scarce and teachers were the only source. you needed a building because the books were in the building. you needed a schedule because the teacher could only be in one place at one time. you needed cohorts because it was impossible to personalize instruction for every student. these constraints are no longer true. information is abundant. teachers are abundant in recorded form. personalization is possible with software. the architecture of school persists because it is an institution, not because it is optimal for learning.

liemandt's point is not that teachers are unnecessary. it is that the container has become the constraint. a great teacher in a fifty-minute period with thirty students is still limited by the period and the headcount. the same teacher working one-on-one or in a small group, with the student advancing at their own pace, is operating in a different universe of effectiveness. the teacher is the same. the student is the same. the physics of attention and time and feedback loops are what change.

the twenty-two hour figure is also an indictment of curriculum design. most school curricula are built around coverage, not mastery. the goal is to expose students to as many topics as possible before the end of the year. depth is sacrificed for breadth because breadth is easier to measure on a standardized test. a student who has seen photosynthesis, the water cycle, the rock cycle, cell division, and genetics in a single semester has been exposed to a lot of science. a student who has spent twenty-two hours deeply understanding photosynthesis, testing predictions, observing results, and correcting misconceptions has actually learned science. the first student will remember fragments for the test and forget them by summer. the second student will still understand photosynthesis in ten years.

the counterargument is that school is not just about academic content. it is about socialization, structure, routine, learning to operate in groups, learning to follow schedules, learning to deal with authority. these are real skills. but they are not the same as learning science, and they do not require two hundred hours of science instruction to achieve. a student could learn social skills in a structured social environment and learn science in a competency-based academic environment. the conflation of the two is a convenience for the institution, not a benefit for the student.

the practical implication is that anyone designing education technology or alternative schooling should focus on removing the constraints that the traditional system added for logistical reasons. if the constraint was teacher availability, use recorded instruction plus live tutoring. if the constraint was standardization, use adaptive software. if the constraint was physical proximity, use remote learning. if the constraint was cohort pacing, let students advance individually. the goal is not to replace teachers with software. it is to remove the parts of the system that were never about learning in the first place.

liemandt's twenty-two hours is a provocation, not a precise measurement. the exact number will vary by student, by subject, by motivation, by prior knowledge. but the order of magnitude is what matters. if the range is not ten to one but five to one, or even three to one, the conclusion is the same. we are spending multiples of the necessary time because the system was built for constraints that no longer exist. the student was never the problem. the student was always capable of learning faster than the system allowed. the system just had other priorities.
